Abstract: Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks

Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across a wide
range of natural language processing tasks. However, their knowledge is frozen at
training time and they are prone to generating factually incorrect statements —
a phenomenon commonly referred to as "hallucination" — particularly on tasks that
require specific, up-to-date, or domain-specific knowledge. Retrieval-Augmented
Generation (RAG) addresses these limitations by coupling a parametric language model
with a non-parametric memory in the form of a dense vector store.

In this paper, we present a comprehensive empirical study of RAG systems across five
knowledge-intensive benchmarks: Natural Questions, TriviaQA, WebQuestions, CuratedTREC,
and a novel enterprise document QA dataset we introduce. We evaluate three retrieval
strategies — BM25 sparse retrieval, dense passage retrieval (DPR), and a hybrid
re-ranking approach — and examine how retrieval quality affects downstream generation
quality as measured by exact match, F1, and factual consistency scores.

Our key findings are as follows. First, retrieval quality is the dominant factor in
end-to-end RAG performance: improving retrieval recall from 60% to 80% yields a larger
accuracy gain than upgrading the generator from a 7B to a 13B parameter model. Second,
the hybrid retrieval approach outperforms either sparse or dense retrieval alone across
all five benchmarks, with an average F1 improvement of 4.3 points over the next-best
single method. Third, chunk size is a critical but often overlooked hyperparameter:
128-token chunks consistently outperform 512-token chunks on precision-sensitive tasks,
while longer chunks are preferred for tasks requiring broader context.

We also introduce a new metric, Retrieval-Attribution Score (RAS), which measures the
degree to which model outputs can be traced back to retrieved passages. High-RAS models
are significantly less likely to produce hallucinated facts, suggesting that attribution
tracking is a practical proxy for factual reliability. We release our benchmark datasets,
evaluation code, and model checkpoints to support reproducibility and further research.

These results have practical implications for practitioners deploying RAG systems in
production. We recommend a retrieval-first optimization strategy, careful tuning of
chunk size to the task domain, and integration of attribution tracking into evaluation
pipelines. Future work will examine the interaction between retrieval strategy and
instruction tuning, and explore real-time retrieval from continuously updated sources.
