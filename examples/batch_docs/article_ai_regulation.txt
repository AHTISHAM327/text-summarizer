European Parliament Passes Landmark AI Liability Directive

The European Parliament voted 412 to 189 on Wednesday to pass the AI Liability
Directive, a landmark piece of legislation that establishes a legal framework for
assigning fault and awarding damages in cases where artificial intelligence systems
cause harm. The directive, which will now proceed to formal adoption by the Council
of the EU before taking effect, is expected to reshape how companies develop, deploy,
and insure AI products across the bloc.

Under the directive, victims of AI-related harm — including discrimination in hiring,
biased credit decisions, or physical injury caused by an autonomous system — will be
able to seek compensation through national courts without needing to prove exactly
how the AI system made a particular decision. This "presumption of causality" clause
was the most contentious provision during three years of legislative negotiations, with
technology industry groups arguing it would make AI development in Europe unworkable
and consumer rights advocates arguing it was essential given the opacity of many
modern systems.

The directive applies to a broad range of AI applications but imposes the strictest
obligations on "high-risk" systems as defined by the parallel EU AI Act, including
systems used in critical infrastructure, law enforcement, education, and employment.
Providers of high-risk AI must maintain detailed technical documentation and logs
sufficient to support litigation, and must disclose this evidence to courts upon request.

Tech companies including several major US platform providers have warned that compliance
costs could run into hundreds of millions of euros annually and that some products may
be withdrawn from the European market. EU officials have dismissed these warnings as
lobbying pressure, pointing to similar predictions that did not materialize after
the General Data Protection Regulation took effect in 2018.

Member states will have 24 months to transpose the directive into national law.
