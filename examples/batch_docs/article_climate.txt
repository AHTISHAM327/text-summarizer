Global Carbon Emissions Hit Record High in 2025

Global carbon dioxide emissions reached a new record of 37.4 billion tonnes in 2025,
according to a report released Tuesday by the Global Carbon Project, a consortium of
international researchers. The figure represents a 1.3 percent increase over 2024
levels and marks the fourth consecutive annual record, underscoring the widening gap
between international climate commitments and real-world progress.

The increase was driven primarily by continued growth in coal consumption in South
and Southeast Asia, where several large economies are expanding power generation
capacity to meet surging electricity demand. China's emissions grew by 0.9 percent,
India's by 4.2 percent, and emissions in Southeast Asia collectively rose by 5.7
percent. Meanwhile, the European Union posted a 3.1 percent reduction, and the
United States held roughly flat, down 0.4 percent.

Renewable energy deployment has accelerated — solar capacity additions hit a new
global record for the fifth year running — but researchers warn that growth in clean
energy is still being outpaced by growth in overall energy demand. "We're adding
renewables fast, but we're adding demand faster," said Dr. Lena Fischer, lead author
of the report. "Until energy efficiency improves significantly, we'll keep burning
more fossil fuels even as the share from renewables grows."

The report estimates that staying within the 1.5 degree Celsius warming target set
by the Paris Agreement would require global emissions to fall by approximately 7
percent per year through 2030. The current trajectory implies warming of around 2.7
degrees by the end of the century.
