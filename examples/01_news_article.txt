City Council Approves Major Downtown Redevelopment Plan

The city council voted 7-2 on Tuesday evening to approve a sweeping downtown redevelopment
plan that will transform nearly 40 acres of underutilized land near the waterfront. The
$240 million project, backed by a consortium of private investors and a $60 million
federal infrastructure grant, is expected to break ground in early spring.

The plan includes the construction of 1,200 mixed-income housing units, of which 30 percent
will be designated as affordable housing for residents earning below 80 percent of the
area median income. Alongside the residential development, the project calls for
150,000 square feet of retail and commercial space, a new 2.5-acre public park along
the riverfront, dedicated cycling infrastructure connecting to the broader city network,
and a relocated transit hub that will serve three bus lines.

Supporters of the plan, including Mayor Diana Reyes, argued that the project addresses
a longstanding housing shortage while revitalizing a neglected part of the city.
"This is the kind of investment our residents have been waiting for," she said at
Tuesday's meeting. "We are building a more equitable and connected city."

Critics, however, raised concerns about the potential displacement of existing low-income
tenants in the surrounding neighborhoods, where rents have already risen sharply in
anticipation of redevelopment. Community organizer Rafael Santos called the affordable
housing provisions "woefully inadequate," arguing that 30 percent falls short of what
is needed to prevent gentrification. He also questioned whether the transit improvements
would be completed before new residents moved in.

The council imposed several conditions on approval: developers must submit a detailed
anti-displacement plan within 90 days, construction must adhere to a strict noise
ordinance protecting nearby residents, and the city retains the right to audit project
finances annually. A community benefits agreement, to be negotiated over the next
six months, will govern local hiring requirements and small business set-asides.

Environmental groups gave the plan a mixed reception. The Sierra Club chapter praised
the cycling infrastructure and green space but noted the project's environmental impact
statement did not adequately assess stormwater runoff risks given the site's proximity
to the river. City engineers say a supplemental study will be completed before permits
are issued.

Construction is projected to take four years, with the first residential units available
for occupancy by late 2028. The city estimates the completed development will generate
approximately $18 million in annual tax revenue and create around 800 permanent jobs.
